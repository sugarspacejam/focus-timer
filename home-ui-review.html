Current first-screen UI is clear, but it will break down once the user depends on it all day.

The goal is to protect the app’s fast, no-calendar, no-heavy-search identity while making it usable for someone who relies on it every day.

Current UX risk: the page asks the user to scroll through creation, task management, and settings in one long lane. That’s fine for a new user with 3 tasks. It becomes slow and mentally noisy for a user with 18–40 recurring tasks.

Recommended redesign direction

Keep the no-calendar, fast-contract identity. Do not turn this into a project manager. The fix is not more advanced filters. The fix is better structure: a compact task launcher, a dedicated task workspace, and clearer separation between “start now” and “manage backlog”.

Concept A: Quick Start + Task Drawer

Best if the product promise is speed. The first screen becomes a launcher, not a long management page.

Recommended
  • Top card becomes one compact composer with a single text field and one main CTA.
  • Saved tasks move into a collapsible drawer or bottom sheet called Task Library.
  • User sees recent / pinned / unfinished tasks first, not an endless stack.
  • Task management actions live inside the drawer, not in the main home scroll.

Concept B: Split home into Today / Tasks / Settings tabs

Best if you expect repeat daily use with many saved tasks and want a more native iPhone information architecture.

More scalable
  • Today: stats, quick start, resume latest tasks.
  • Tasks: dedicated browsing and editing view.
  • Settings: voice mode, camera, pro, account.
  • No advanced search required; simple segments like Pinned / Recent / All are enough.

This reduces vertical fatigue and preserves speed. A user with many tasks stops feeling punished for having history.

Concept C: Task stacks instead of flat list

Best if your users tend to repeat the same few contexts across a week.

Behavioral fit
  • Instead of full search, group tasks into tiny smart stacks: Recently used, Frequently failed, Needs finishing, Pinned.
  • This makes retrieval feel intelligent without asking the user for dates or complex filters.
  • Paying users care less about raw search power and more about getting to the next contract in under 2 seconds.

Story: what a real paying user of this app looks like

The purpose of this story is not marketing. It is to simulate repeated real use and surface the annoying things that only show up after the app becomes part of someone’s actual life.

Nadia, 34, product lead, remote, overloaded, willing to pay to stop slipping

Nadia doesn’t need another planner. She already has calendars, Slack, Linear, Notes, and 40 browser tabs. What she is missing is a tool that gets her over the hump between intention and action. She pays for Done in 5 because it gives her a small, confrontational start ritual. She opens it when she feels friction, avoidance, or task-switching shame.

Monday, 9:12 AM

Nadia unlocks her phone after replying to messages she didn’t want to answer. She knows exactly what she should do next: finish the launch memo. She opens Done in 5 because she wants one small contract, not a plan for the week.

“I don’t want to organize my work. I want to start.”

What happens with the current UI

She lands on a page with stats, task composer, task list, and settings all in one scroll. It looks good. But she has 24 saved tasks now. Some are real work, some are errands, some are repeated admin tasks she keeps reusing. She has to scroll. The task list is long. Every row has Start, Edit, Delete. The screen starts to feel like a maintenance page instead of a launch pad.

If she wants to quickly reuse an existing task, she has to visually scan rows. If she wants to create a new one, the composer is there, but it sits above a long list that keeps reminding her of everything else she is not doing.

Tuesday, 2:47 PM

Nadia is tired. The app matters most now, not when she is fresh. She reaches for it during the low-discipline part of the day. This is when little annoyances become lethal: too much scrolling, too many repeated controls, too much visual sameness between “create”, “browse”, and “settings”.

She does not need a calendar. She does not need to tag tasks. She does not want to manage a system. She wants the home screen to ask one sharp question: what are you committing to right now?

Why she pays

Not for storage. Not for statistics alone. She pays because the app helps her cross the activation barrier several times a day. That means the premium experience should reduce hesitation, reduce scanning, and reduce the chance of drifting into list-management behavior.

What annoys Nadia after 3 weeks

She notices that saved tasks don’t feel organized by actual use. The same important recurring tasks should be instantly available. Instead, the system treats everything as one flat list. Editing and deleting are visible on every row even though most of the time she only wants to start a task. The home screen becomes crowded with capabilities she uses rarely, while the one thing she uses constantly — starting fast — gets less dominant over time.

What the story reveals

1. The home screen should optimize for commitment, not management

  • Start now should dominate.
  • Task maintenance should be one layer away.
  • The first screen should feel lighter as task count grows, not heavier.

2. Flat task rows are okay for 5 tasks, bad for 25

  • Repeated Start / Edit / Delete controls create noise.
  • Search is not enough if the real issue is poor structure.
  • Pinned / Recent / Reuse patterns are better than advanced filtering.

3. Premium users probably want reuse speed more than feature depth

  • “Let me restart yesterday’s important task instantly.”
  • “Show me the few things I actually contract with all the time.”
  • “Don’t make me manage a catalog when I’m already procrastinating.”

4. The best next UI move

  • Make home a compact quick-start surface.
  • Move full task browsing to a drawer, sheet, or separate tab.
  • Introduce Pinned / Recent / All instead of full calendar/search systems.
